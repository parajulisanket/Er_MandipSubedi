About

A Lifelong Journey in Engineering and Service

From Rolpa to Nepal’s river valleys and fault lines, I’ve focused on turning evidence into protection—mapping liquefaction risk for Kathmandu, leading rapid post-quake fieldwork in Jajarkot, and helping design resilient bridge foundations so infrastructure survives the next shock.

Much of my work lives in the field and the lab: after the 3 November 2023 Jajarkot earthquake, our rapid reconnaissance documented structural failures and geotechnical effects to guide safer rebuilding. Earlier, I mapped liquefaction hazard and ground-failure probability across the Kathmandu Valley using a large borehole dataset to support planning and policy.

On the design side, I’ve contributed to bridge projects with foundations tuned to Himalayan rivers—including a composite well-pile foundation case study—and focus on turning assessments into practical standards and training. I currently serve as President of the Nepal Geotechnical Society and teach as an associate professor at Universal Engineering & Science College, where mentoring the next cohort turns research into capacity.

“The problems engineers face in Nepal are not technical alone — they are institutional, social, and systemic. That’s where I focus my energy.” — Er. Mandip Subedi

Institutional Leadership

Strengthening Engineering from Within

These roles were opportunities to listen, build platforms, and push for systems that protect and empower engineers — especially those working in difficult terrain, with limited support, and against time. Through collaborative leadership and advocacy, I aimed to create pathways for professional growth, encourage innovation, and ensure the voices of all engineers—both established and emerging—were heard and valued.
